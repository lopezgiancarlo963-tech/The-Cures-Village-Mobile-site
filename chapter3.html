CHAPTER 3

The Shifting Shadows

The shifting shadows around him aren’t just formless shapes—they seem to be forming into specific images. One shadow takes the shape of his village, its streets empty and desolate, with sickened villagers stumbling about. Another shifts into the form of a creature, a twisted, monstrous version of something he might have once known.

The green flame flickers in the brazier, and it’s clear that these images are connected to Brulgwyn’s past and fears. They’re manifesting his deepest anxieties, specifically the fear of losing his village, his people, and his sense of duty.

Eldrin’s voice echoes again. “What will you do when your greatest fear confronts you? Will you run, or will you face it head-on?”

With a 17 on your Religion check, Brulgwyn senses that the trial is deeply rooted in spiritual and arcane forces. This is not just an illusion—there is something ancient at work here. The green flame in the brazier is a conduit, channeling eldritch magic, likely tied to an old druidic or fey power.

The shadows forming around him are more than just figments of his mind. They are manifestations of his fears, shaped by whatever force governs this trial. However, with his knowledge of religious and mystical rites, Brulgwyn realizes something crucial.

This test is not about strength alone. It is about acceptance—facing his fears, not simply fighting them. If he only relies on his wooden hammer, he may fail. But if he acknowledges and endures the fear, he may overcome the trial.

Brulgwyn plants his feet firmly on the ground, gripping the handle of his wooden hammer but not raising it. He takes a deep breath, steadying himself as the shadowy visions swirl around him—his village in ruin, his people suffering, and the twisted creature lurking at the edge of the dark.

He does not flinch.

The desperate cries of his people echo in his ears, their hollow eyes pleading for salvation. The monster snarls, its form writhing with unnatural energy, waiting to pounce. But Brulgwyn does not run, nor does he strike.

Instead, he accepts what he sees.

His people need him, but fear will not save them. The creature is a reflection of his doubts, not an enemy he must kill. Brulgwyn steadies his heart, knowing that no warrior wins every battle through steel alone.

The moment he makes peace with his fear, the shadows tremble—and then begin to dissolve. The village fades into mist. The monstrous creature lets out one final, haunting growl before vanishing into the ether.

Silence.

The green flame in the brazier flares one last time before dimming.

Eldrin Thornbrew steps forward, nodding approvingly. “You understand now.” His voice carries a hint of respect. “Courage is not the absence of fear, but the will to face it.”

He gestures toward Brulgwyn. “You have passed the trial. And so, I will help you.”

Brulgwyn has overcome his fears and earned Eldrin’s aid.

Brulgwyn straightens himself, the weight of the trial still settling in his mind. He looks Eldrin in the eye and gives a firm nod.

“I thank ye for this trial,” he says, his voice steady. “I see now that strength alone won’t be enough to save my people. I’ll remember this.”

Eldrin watches him for a moment, then gives a slow nod of approval. “Gratitude is a rare thing among warriors. Many would curse the test instead of learning from it.” His lips curl into the faintest smirk. “You may yet have the wisdom to succeed in your quest.”

He turns toward the brazier and waves a hand over the embers. The green flame flickers once more, and a faint golden glow shimmers in the air.

“Come,” he says. “You have earned my aid, and I will tell you what you seek.”

Brulgwyn follows as Eldrin steps beyond the altar and toward a small wooden hut hidden in the trees. Inside, shelves lined with ancient tomes, dried herbs, and strange trinkets fill the space with the scent of earth and time itself. Eldrin gestures for him to sit.

“The flu that plagues your people…” Eldrin murmurs, pulling a worn leather-bound book from the shelf. “It is no ordinary sickness.” He places the book before Brulgwyn and flips it open. The pages reveal drawings of withered bodies, dark veins twisting like roots beneath their skin.

“This illness,” Eldrin continues, “is not a disease of nature. It is a curse.”

The words hang heavy in the air.

With a 5 on Insight, Brulgwyn struggles to read Eldrin’s expression. The old mystic’s face remains unreadable, his tone calm and steady. There’s no clear sign of deceit, but Brulgwyn can’t tell if Eldrin is holding anything back or if the word "curse" carries more weight than it seems.

One thing is certain—the drawings in the book bear an eerie resemblance to what Brulgwyn saw in his village. The darkened veins, the weakness in his people… it matches what Eldrin describes. Whether he fully trusts the man or not, there’s a strong chance he knows something valuable.
